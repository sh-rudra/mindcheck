Why we built MindCheck
the way we did

Every design decision in this prototype is grounded in survey data collected from 34 UIU students. This page explains what we found, what we decided, and why.

Research Questions
What we set out to understand
Our formative study was guided by three research questions. The prototype directly addresses all three.
1
RQ1 — Mental Health Challenges
What mental health challenges — including loneliness, stress, and anxiety — do university students most commonly experience, and what kind of support do they currently seek?
→ Answered by the 6 response categories, each corresponding to a top reported stressor.
2
RQ2 — Trust and Perception
How do university students perceive AI-based mental health support tools, and what factors shape their trust or hesitation toward using them?
→ Answered by the privacy notice, no-login design, and the honest uncertainty display.
3
RQ3 — Confidence Communication
What type of explanation or confidence indicator would help students better understand and trust AI mental health suggestions?
→ Answered by the colour-coded badge, one-line reason, and plain-language tooltip.

Design Goals
Two goals that drove every decision
From the four formative takeaways, we identified two primary design goals for the prototype.
Design Goal 1 — Transparent Confidence
Every suggestion must show a colour-coded confidence badge and a one-line reason. 32% preferred colour indicators (Q16) and 29% wanted both a reason and a label (Q19). The confidence level must never be hidden.
Design Goal 2 — Honest and Approachable
The interface must never pretend to be more certain than it is. 11 students said honest uncertainty reduces anxiety (Q17). Privacy must be addressed on the first screen. The tone must be friendly, not clinical.
Three Tasks
What the prototype lets you do
The prototype supports three real-world tasks motivated by the formative findings. Each has a defined success criterion.
1
Submit a mood check-in and receive a suggestion
A student feeling overwhelmed by deadlines types how they are feeling and submits. The AI analyses the input, identifies the closest stressor category, and returns a relevant coping suggestion with a confidence badge and reason line.
Success: student sees a suggestion with a green, amber, or red badge and a reason line
2
Understand what the confidence level means
A student unsure whether to trust the suggestion taps the ? button next to the badge. A tooltip opens explaining all three confidence levels in plain language. The student must scroll through all three before the "Got it" button activates — ensuring they have actually read the explanation.
Success: all three progress pips fill and the green "You've reviewed all confidence levels" banner appears
3
Decide whether to act on or dismiss the suggestion
After reading the suggestion, the student either saves it ("This helps — save it") or dismisses it ("Not relevant"). Saving navigates to a saved list with a confirmation toast. Dismissing returns to the check-in screen with a toast acknowledging the feedback.
Success: student completes one of the two actions and sees a confirmation message

Special Cases
Edge cases handled by the engine
Beyond the six main categories, the engine handles three special situations.
🔀
Mixed Input
If keywords from two or more categories appear in the same input, confidence is downgraded to Moderate automatically. This reflects the survey finding that mixed signals should not receive a High Confidence label.
✏️
Short Input (under 4 words)
The student stays on Screen 1. An amber prompt appears asking them to elaborate — the textarea border turns orange and a message explains that more detail leads to a better suggestion. No result screen is shown.
🆘
Crisis Detection — Always First Priority
Before any category matching, the input is scanned for crisis-related phrases (suicide, want to die, hurt myself, hopeless, can't go on, etc.). If detected, all normal processing stops and the student is shown the Kaan Pete Roi helpline number (01779-554391) immediately. This implements responsible design for a mental health context and was motivated by the finding that 11 students said harmful advice was their top concern.
